Foundations — What Business Analysts Actually Do

The BA role is one of the most misunderstood in tech and operations. Before we go deep on techniques, we need to settle what a BA actually is, where the confusion comes from, and why getting it wrong costs projects dearly.

The Note-taker Myth

Ask ten people at a company what a business analyst does, and you will get ten different answers. In some organisations the BA is the person who sits in meetings and types everything into Confluence. In others, the BA is a junior project manager who helps with scheduling. In a few, the BA is mistaken for a data analyst — someone who builds dashboards in Excel or Power BI.

None of these are wrong, exactly. But none of them are right, either. These misunderstandings persist because the BA role sits at the intersection of business, technology, and people — which means it is easy for each of those groups to pull the role toward their own definition.

The consequence is that requirements are gathered badly, which means projects fail — not at deployment, but at the moment someone first sat down and said "tell me what you need."

Field Note

At a garment factory in Chattogram I joined a project that had already burned three months. The "BA" on record had produced a 47-page document of what people said they wanted — meeting notes, email summaries, whiteboard photos. What was missing: a single sentence about what the system needed to do. The developer had been building against the wrong assumptions for weeks. The real business requirement — track shipment status by buyer PO — was buried in paragraph 31.

A business analyst is the person responsible for ensuring that the right problem is solved. Not the stated problem. Not the politically popular problem. The actual problem that, if solved, would create value for the organisation and its users.

This means the BA's primary output is not a document. It is shared understanding — between stakeholders, between business and IT, between what was asked and what needs to be built.

BA vs PM vs SA: The Hybrid Reality in Bangladesh

Internationally, the roles are reasonably distinct. The Business Analyst defines what to build. The Project Manager defines when and how much. The Systems Analyst or Solution Architect defines how it will be built technically.

In Bangladesh's technology and consulting market, however, one person often does all three. Smaller teams, tighter budgets, and a culture of "wear multiple hats" means you may carry the BA title while also tracking timelines and reviewing technical design documents.

This is not a problem — as long as you know which hat you are wearing at any given moment. The danger is conflating them: letting PM urgency pressure you to stop asking important BA questions, or letting SA thinking bias you toward solutions before you have fully understood the problem.

Role Core question Primary output
Business Analyst What does the business actually need? Requirements, user stories, specs
Project Manager How do we deliver this, by when, for how much? Plan, risk register, status reports
Systems Analyst / SA How should the system be built to meet these needs? Technical design, architecture, data model

When you are doing all three jobs, create explicit checkpoints: "Right now I am doing requirements work. I will not let delivery pressure shorten this." That discipline is what separates BAs who produce good outcomes from those who produce fast-but-wrong ones.

Why Requirements Fail: A Taxonomy of Common Breakdowns

The Chaos Report by the Standish Group has tracked software project failure since 1994. Requirements-related problems are consistently in the top three causes of project failure globally. In Bangladesh, where many projects run without a dedicated BA, the number is higher.

Here is the failure taxonomy I have built from project post-mortems in the RMG, financial services, and NGO sectors:

Stated ≠ Real

Stakeholders say what they think you want to hear, or what sounds impressive, rather than what they actually need. A factory MD says "full visibility" when what he needs is one alert when a shipment is delayed.

Missing stakeholders

Requirements are gathered from the people in the room, not the people who will use the system. Floor supervisors are never consulted about the system they will use every day.

Ambiguity accepted

The BA hears "the system should be fast" and writes it down without pressing for a definition. Fast means under 2 seconds to one stakeholder and under 2 minutes to another.

Scope creep by silence

Nobody defines what is out of scope. Six months in, the MD asks why the system does not handle export documentation — a feature nobody ever agreed to build.

Requirements as wish lists

Requirements are collected but never prioritised. The team builds everything equally, running out of time before completing the core features the business actually needed.

No traceability

Requirements change during development but nobody updates the spec. By UAT, nobody can remember what was agreed, and sign-off becomes a negotiation, not a verification.

Understanding these failure modes is the first step to avoiding them. The rest of this course addresses each one directly.

What Good Looks Like: The BA's North Star

A well-executed requirements phase produces a small set of outcomes. Not documents — outcomes. The documents are evidence of the outcomes, not the outcomes themselves.

  1. Shared understanding — Every stakeholder and every developer has the same mental model of what is being built and why.
  2. Testable requirements — Every requirement can be unambiguously verified as met or not met during UAT. "The user can view their shipment status" is testable. "The system provides good visibility" is not.
  3. Prioritised scope — Everyone agrees on what is in, what is out, and in what order things will be delivered.
  4. Documented decisions — Every major decision about scope, design, or priority is recorded with the reason. When a stakeholder later asks "why did we do it this way?" you have the answer.
"The BA's job is not to write things down. It is to create conditions under which the right thing gets built."

Bangladesh Context: BAs in RMG & Supply Chain Projects

Bangladesh's garment and export sector has unique characteristics that shape how requirements gathering works. Understanding these will make you more effective here than a consultant parachuted in from a different market.

BD Context

Hierarchy is real and must be respected. In most Bangladeshi factories, the MD or owner has final say on everything. Requirements sessions that go around the MD — even unintentionally — will face resistance. Always get top-level buy-in first, then work down. Never present findings to junior staff before senior leadership has seen them.

Indirect communication is the norm. When a factory manager says "yes, that could work," they often mean "I have concerns but do not want to be the one to say no." Learn to hear the qualifications in polite agreement.

Process knowledge lives in people, not documents. Unlike multinational factories, Bangladeshi SME factories often have no written SOPs. The export documentation process exists entirely in the head of one experienced merchandiser. Your job is to externalise that knowledge before you can digitise it.

Multi-system environments. Many factories run parallel systems — an ERP module, an Excel sheet, a WhatsApp group, and a paper register — for the same process. Mapping what currently exists before prescribing what should exist is essential.

These are not obstacles. They are context. A BA who understands them will ask better questions, surface hidden requirements, and avoid the mistakes that make projects run over time and budget.

Field Notes: The 5 Most Common BA Anti-patterns

These are the behaviours that undermine requirements quality. I have done every single one at some point.

01 — Leading questions

"Wouldn't it be useful if the system could generate reports automatically?" The stakeholder says yes. You now have a requirement that was never a real need — and someone has to build it.

02 — Solution bias

You arrive with the solution in mind and gather requirements to fit it. This is especially common when the BA is also the tech lead. The result is a system that solves the wrong problem elegantly.

03 — The 3am requirement

You gather requirements, write a spec, get sign-off — then receive an email at 11pm three weeks before go-live: "We just realised we need X." If X was not in scope, this is a change request, not a bug. Document the difference.

04 — Proxy stakeholders

You interview the manager who speaks confidently about what their team needs. But you never speak to the actual users. The system launches and nobody uses it because it doesn't fit how the work actually happens.

05 — Analysis paralysis

You keep refining the requirements document instead of releasing it. Perfect becomes the enemy of good. A 90% accurate spec that gets reviewed and signed off is better than a 100% accurate spec that never leaves your desk.

Bonus — Orphaned requirements

Requirements are written, then never linked to designs, test cases, or development tasks. By the time UAT arrives, nobody can tell what was actually agreed. Traceability prevents this.

Template: BA Role Charter

Before starting any project, produce a one-page BA Role Charter and share it with your project sponsor. This prevents the misunderstandings about your role from becoming friction later. Here is the structure:

Template

BA Role Charter — one-page structure

Project: [Name]
BA: [Your name]
Sponsor: [Name & title]

What I will do: Facilitate requirements workshops, document business and functional requirements, create user stories and acceptance criteria, support UAT planning, maintain traceability between requirements and test cases.

What I will not do: Project manage the delivery timeline, make technology architecture decisions, perform user acceptance testing myself, decide business priorities without stakeholder agreement.

My output artefacts: Stakeholder register, requirements specification (SRS-lite), user story backlog with acceptance criteria, UAT plan, traceability matrix.

Key decisions I need from you: Who are the authorised approvers for requirements? What is the change control process? What is the target go-live date?

Agreed on: [Date]   Signed: [Sponsor name]

This document will save you from scope creep, from being blamed for decisions that were not yours, and from the "we never agreed to that" conversation at go-live. It takes 30 minutes to write and pays dividends throughout the project.

Reflection Questions

  1. Think of a project you have been involved in where requirements were unclear or changed significantly. Which failure mode from the taxonomy applied — and at what point could a BA intervention have helped?
  2. On a scale of 1–5, how clearly do the people around you understand what a BA does? What is one thing you could communicate to change that?
  3. Draft the first three lines of a BA Role Charter for a project you are currently working on or have recently completed. What would you list under "what I will not do"?
