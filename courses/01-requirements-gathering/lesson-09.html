The BA Artefacts Checklist: What to Hand Over at Project Close

At the end of a project, the BA's output should be a complete, organised set of documents that allow any future team member — or auditor — to understand what was built, why, and what was agreed. This is not bureaucracy. It is institutional memory.

The following checklist covers every artefact this course has taught you to produce. Check off what you have delivered by the end of a project:

  • BA Role CharterScope of the BA's responsibilities, agreed with the project sponsor
  • Stakeholder RegisterAll stakeholders, their power/interest mapping, RACI roles, communication preferences
  • Workshop agendas and 48-hour summariesOne per session conducted, with decisions, open questions, and next steps
  • Interview notes or summariesStructured notes from all key stakeholder interviews
  • As-is process documentationDocumented current state of the processes the system replaces or changes
  • Business Requirements documentHigh-level goals the project must achieve, signed by the sponsor
  • SRS-lite (Functional Specification)Numbered functional, non-functional, and integration requirements
  • Data dictionaryField-level definitions, types, validations, and rules for every data entity
  • User Story BacklogAll user stories with acceptance criteria, priority, and status
  • Requirements Traceability Matrix (RTM)Links requirements to test cases, delivery sprints, and business objectives
  • Change RegisterAll change requests raised, with decisions, impact assessments, and outcomes
  • UAT PlanScope, schedule, entry/exit criteria, test team, defect process
  • UAT Test ScenariosNumbered test cases linked to requirements and acceptance criteria
  • Defect LogAll defects raised during UAT, severity, resolution, and sign-off status
  • Sign-off MatrixSigned acceptance by module/area from all named approvers
  • Post-Implementation Review notesLessons learned, requirements quality assessment, process improvements for next project
Rule

A document that lives only on your laptop is not a project artefact — it is a personal note. Every artefact must be in a shared, version-controlled location (SharePoint, Google Drive, Confluence) that the project sponsor can access without asking you. If you leave the project tomorrow, the work should survive.

Handover Documentation: Writing for the Person Who Comes After You

The hardest part of handover documentation is that you write it knowing the context that makes everything obvious — so the obvious context gets left out. Write for someone who has never spoken to any stakeholder and never attended any workshop. What do they need to know to take over this project without causing harm?

A good BA handover document includes:

  • Project summary — In three paragraphs: what was being built, why, and where it currently stands
  • Key stakeholder contacts — Name, title, role on the project, and one sentence on how to work with each effectively
  • Document index — Where every artefact lives, what version it is, and what state it is in
  • Open items — Every unresolved requirement, open change request, deferred defect, and pending sign-off. With owner and deadline.
  • Known risks and sensitivities — Interpersonal dynamics, political landmines, technical assumptions that are fragile. These never appear in formal documents but they shape every decision.
  • Glossary — Organisation-specific terms, acronyms, and product names that appear throughout the documentation

Post-Implementation Review: Closing the Requirements Loop

Four to eight weeks after go-live, conduct a post-implementation review (PIR). This is not a blame exercise — it is a learning exercise. The questions to answer:

  • Did the system achieve the business requirements stated at the start of the project? How do we know?
  • Which requirements turned out to be wrong, incomplete, or unnecessary? What would better elicitation have looked like?
  • Which defects found at UAT were preventable with better requirements work?
  • Which change requests were genuine scope gaps (requirements failure) versus legitimate business changes?
  • What would we do differently in the requirements phase on the next project?

The PIR output is a one-page retrospective, shared with the project sponsor and stored with the project artefacts. It is the source of the "field notes" in this course.

Tools BAs Use

JIRA

Industry-standard backlog and issue tracking. Stores user stories, tracks sprints, manages defect logs. Essential for Agile projects. Learning JIRA is non-negotiable for any BA working with a software development team.

Confluence

Atlassian's wiki tool. The de-facto home for requirements documents, meeting notes, and project documentation on teams using JIRA. Integrates tightly with JIRA stories.

Miro / Lucidchart

Online whiteboard tools for process mapping, stakeholder maps, and workshop facilitation. Miro is better for facilitated sessions; Lucidchart is better for polished process flow diagrams.

Figma

The standard tool for UI prototyping. A BA who can produce low-fidelity wireframes in Figma dramatically reduces the gap between written requirements and what developers actually build.

Microsoft Excel / Google Sheets

The most-used BA tool in Bangladesh, across all sectors. RTMs, data dictionaries, UAT logs, and stakeholder registers often live in spreadsheets. Mastery of Excel is foundational.

Visio / draw.io

Process flow diagramming tools. BPMN diagrams, system context diagrams, and data flow diagrams. draw.io (now diagrams.net) is free and integrates with Google Drive.

BD Tool Reality

In most Bangladeshi organisations, the BA toolkit is: Microsoft Word (specs), Excel (everything else), and WhatsApp (all real communication). Do not be snobby about this. A well-structured Excel-based RTM maintained consistently outperforms a poorly maintained JIRA backlog every time.

However: invest in learning at least one diagramming tool (draw.io is free), one backlog tool (JIRA has a free tier), and Figma (free for basic use). These skills differentiate you when working with international clients or on digital transformation projects.

Certifications Worth Pursuing

CBAP

Certified Business Analysis Professional

Offered by IIBA (International Institute of Business Analysis). The gold standard BA certification globally. Requires five years of BA experience. Recognised by international employers and consulting firms. Preparation takes 3–6 months. Exam fee is approximately USD 325 (member) or USD 450 (non-member).

PMI-PBA

PMI Professional in Business Analysis

Offered by the Project Management Institute. Bridges BA and PM disciplines — well-suited for the Bangladeshi market where the BA-PM hybrid role is common. Requires a bachelor's degree plus experience. Pairs well with PMP.

ECBA / CCBA

Entry/Intermediate IIBA Certifications

ECBA (Entry Certificate) requires no prior experience — good for professionals transitioning into BA work. CCBA (Capability) requires 3.75 years experience. Both use the BABOK (Business Analysis Body of Knowledge) as the curriculum, which is worth reading even without certification.

Agile BA

Agile Business Analysis Certifications

IIBA-AACBA (Agile Analysis Certification) and the SAFe POPM (SAFe Product Owner/Product Manager) are increasingly relevant as Bangladeshi tech firms adopt Agile delivery. These signal comfort with user story-centric requirements work and sprint-based delivery.

Career Paths for BAs in Bangladesh

Path 01

Corporate / In-house

BA at a bank, telco, conglomerate, or large RMG group. Stable salary, structured environment. Progression: BA → Senior BA → Business Systems Lead → IT Manager or Process Excellence Head. Best for: building deep domain knowledge in one industry. Salary range: BDT 40,000–150,000/month depending on company and seniority.

Path 02

Consulting

BA at an ERP or digital transformation consultancy (local or multinational). Higher variation, faster skill development, exposure to multiple industries. Progression: BA → Senior BA → Functional Consultant → Solution Architect or Engagement Lead. Best for: breadth and career acceleration. Salary: BDT 50,000–200,000/month plus bonuses.

Path 03

Freelance / Independent

Independent BA serving SMEs, startups, or international clients via platforms (Upwork, Toptal) or direct relationships. High earning potential (USD 40–80/hour on international platforms), high variability. Best for: experienced BAs (3+ years) with a track record and self-marketing ability. Requires business development discipline.

International Applicability

The skills in this course are not Bangladesh-specific. Requirements gathering, stakeholder management, user story writing, and UAT facilitation are globally applicable disciplines used in every technology project worldwide.

What Bangladesh specifically taught you — how to navigate hierarchical organisations, how to elicit requirements when documentation does not exist, how to run workshops with mixed-capability participants, how to work across language and cultural gaps — is a premium skill set in international consulting. These are the environments that consultants from more documented, lower-context cultures struggle with. You have trained in the hard mode. That is an advantage.

How These Skills Travel Internationally

If you want to work internationally — in the UK, UAE, Singapore, or elsewhere — the CBAP certification is your entry credential. Pair it with documented project experience (specific industries, system types, project outcomes), a portfolio of anonymised artefacts, and English business communication skills. The combination is competitive in any market.

Bangladeshi BAs who successfully transition internationally typically find two advantages: they are comfortable with ambiguity (because they have rarely had the luxury of well-defined processes); and they are used to building consensus across very different stakeholder personalities, which is a skill that Western-trained BAs often lack.

Your Next Steps: Building Your BA Portfolio

A BA portfolio is a curated collection of anonymised artefacts that demonstrates your capabilities to a potential employer or client. Start building it now, even if you are in your first BA role:

  1. Anonymise and save your best artefacts — Remove company names and sensitive data. Keep the structure, the quality of thinking, and the techniques. A strong stakeholder register and RTM from a real project are more compelling than any certification.
  2. Document your projects — Maintain a project log: industry, system type, your role, key techniques used, outcome. Even if each entry is one paragraph, this becomes the basis for interview answers and proposal writing.
  3. Write about what you have learned — A LinkedIn article about one BA technique you found effective on a real project demonstrates more credibility than a resume bullet point. Write one article per quarter.
  4. Join IIBA Bangladesh chapter — Connect with other BAs, access events and learning resources, and start building the professional network that generates referrals.
  5. Find a mentor — One experienced BA who will review your work honestly and give you specific feedback is worth ten certifications. Ask explicitly. Most experienced practitioners are willing to mentor someone who is serious.
